Frequently Asked Questions

This page answers common questions about the Whytegod Protocol in direct, non-marketing language.

What is Whytegod?

Whytegod is a neutral protocol for creating and verifying permanent records using cryptographic hashes and public anchors.

What problem does it solve?

It proves that specific data existed, unchanged, at or before a known point in time — without storing the data itself.

Is Whytegod a blockchain?

No. Whytegod does not run a chain or consensus. It relies on existing immutable public systems for anchoring.

What is a WRID?

A WRID (Whytegod Record ID) is a SHA-256 hash derived from original data. It uniquely represents that data.

Does Whytegod store my data?

No. The protocol never stores user data, files, identities, or metadata. Only hashes are used.

What does “anchoring” mean?

Anchoring means publishing a WRID to an immutable public system such as Bitcoin, Ethereum, or a public log.

What makes a record valid?

  • The hash is deterministic
  • The anchor is immutable
  • The timestamp is verifiable

Can records be changed or deleted?

No. Once anchored, a record cannot be altered or revoked.

What is WGD?

WGD is a utility token used to introduce cost and friction when anchoring records.

Is WGD an investment or security?

No. WGD provides no ownership, profit rights, or governance power.

Do I need WGD to verify records?

No. Verification is free and permissionless. WGD is only used when anchoring new records.

Who controls the protocol?

No one. Whytegod has no governance, administrators, or privileged actors.

Can the protocol rules change?

No. The protocol is frozen per version. Any changes require a new version.

What does verification actually prove?

It proves data integrity and existence in time. It does not prove ownership, truth, or intent.

Whytegod defines cryptographic facts only. Interpretation is external to the protocol.
